# Economics Formula Reference

## Microeconomics

**Elasticities**
- Price elasticity of demand: `E_d = (%ΔQd) / (%ΔP)` — elastic if |E_d| > 1, inelastic if < 1.
- Midpoint (arc) elasticity: `E = [(Q2-Q1)/((Q2+Q1)/2)] / [(P2-P1)/((P2+P1)/2)]` — use when comparing two discrete points, avoids the "which direction" ambiguity of point elasticity.
- Income elasticity of demand: `E_y = (%ΔQd) / (%ΔIncome)` — negative = inferior good, positive = normal good, >1 = luxury.
- Cross-price elasticity: `E_xy = (%ΔQd,x) / (%ΔP,y)` — positive = substitutes, negative = complements.

**Firm behavior**
- Marginal revenue: `MR = ΔTR/ΔQ`. Profit-maximizing output: `MR = MC`.
- Perfect competition: `P = MR = MC` for the firm; long-run `P = MC = ATC` (zero economic profit).
- Monopoly: set `MR = MC` to find Q, then read price off the demand curve at that Q (price always exceeds MC for a monopolist).
- Shutdown rule (short run): produce only if `P ≥ AVC`.

**Surplus and efficiency**
- Consumer surplus = area between demand curve and price, above price line.
- Producer surplus = area between price and supply curve, below price line.
- Deadweight loss = area of the triangle between supply and demand curves over the quantity range that's no longer traded (e.g., due to a tax, price ceiling/floor, or monopoly underproduction).
- Tax incidence: the side of the market with the *less elastic* curve bears more of the tax burden, regardless of which side is legally taxed.

**Comparative advantage**
- Opportunity cost of good X = amount of good Y given up to produce one more unit of X.
- A country/person has comparative advantage in the good for which their opportunity cost is *lower* — trade is mutually beneficial as long as the agreed price falls between the two parties' opportunity costs.

## Macroeconomics

**National accounts**
- GDP (expenditure approach): `Y = C + I + G + (X − M)`.
- Real GDP = Nominal GDP / GDP deflator × 100.
- GDP deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100.
- Inflation rate (CPI-based) = `(CPI_this_year − CPI_last_year) / CPI_last_year × 100`.

**Labor market**
- Unemployment rate = Unemployed / Labor force × 100.
- Labor force participation rate = Labor force / Working-age population × 100.
- Okun's law (rule of thumb): each 1 percentage point rise in unemployment above the natural rate is associated with roughly a 2% drop in real GDP relative to potential.

**Money and prices**
- Quantity theory of money: `M × V = P × Y` (money supply × velocity = price level × real output).
- Money multiplier (simple) = `1 / required reserve ratio`.
- Fisher equation: `nominal rate ≈ real rate + expected inflation`.

**Short-run models**
- Phillips curve: inverse short-run relationship between inflation and unemployment; shifts right (worse trade-off) with a rise in expected inflation or a negative supply shock.
- IS curve: combinations of interest rate and output where goods market is in equilibrium (Y = C+I+G+NX); shifts right with expansionary fiscal policy.
- LM curve: combinations of interest rate and output where money market is in equilibrium (money demand = money supply); shifts right with expansionary monetary policy.
- AD-AS: aggregate demand shifts with C, I, G, NX, or M; short-run aggregate supply shifts with input costs/supply shocks; long-run aggregate supply is vertical at potential GDP.

**Open economy**
- Real exchange rate = Nominal exchange rate × (Domestic price level / Foreign price level).
- Purchasing power parity (PPP): in the long run, exchange rates adjust so identical goods cost the same across countries — a currency is "undervalued" if PPP implies it should be stronger.
